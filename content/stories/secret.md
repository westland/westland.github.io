The oldest trade secret still under protection by US law is Merchandise 7X – the ‘secret ingredient’ in CocaCola, which has remained a secret since its invention in 1886. In 1925, the only written copy of its formula Coca-Cola admits to having was retrieved from a New York bank (where it had been held as collateral on a sugar loan) and reverently laid in a safe deposit box in SunTrust Bank in Atlanta. That same year the company set a policy whereby no one could view the formula without written permission from the Board, and then only in the presence of the President, Chairman or Corporate Secretary. Furthermore, the rule dictated that only two company officials would be allowed to know the recipe at any given time, and their identities were never to be disclosed for any reason. The company has admitted to

tinkering with the formula over the years (glycerine was added as a preservative, cocaine was eliminated, caffeine was greatly reduced, and citric acid was replaced with phosphoric acid). The formula was also changed in 1935 with the help of Rabbi Tobias Geffen of Atlanta to allow it to be certified kosher. Rumour has it that the 1925 copy of the formula now resides in a safe at CocaCola’s Atlanta headquarters.

Merchandise 7X joins two other trade secrets in being fixtures of popular speculation – McDonald’s secret sauce, and KFC’s secret blend of 11 herbs and spices. KFC’s security measures for the latter include blending those 11 herbs and spices at two different locations and combining at a third location prior to basting the chicken with them
